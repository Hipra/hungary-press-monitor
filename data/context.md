## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as PM on May 10–11, 2026, ending Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a Roma children campaign promise on inauguration day and appointed Hungary's first blind minister. The justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. The first cabinet crisis emerged when Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid following nepotism criticism — a durable counter-narrative in Western outlets. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation, now framed as a deliberate horizontal power-distribution model (Reuters, May 12). Euractiv (May 13) frames the new cabinet as signaling a sharp institutional break with Orbán-era governance. The supermajority is increasingly framed as structural risk, not democratic strength. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat and offered to resign as party chief.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–12, covered across WaPo, BBC, Guardian, DW, Le Monde, Euronews. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Euractiv signals Hungary may raise billions in additional Ukrainian military aid repayments. Hungary confirmed a Paks nuclear plant expansion review. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. NYT reports Magyar's government is scrutinising Orbán's China ties. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved: Reuters (May 11) characterises the goal as "ambitious but possible," while Euractiv (May 7) counters with "this deal is getting worse all the time." S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but flagged euro-adoption steps as a potential reversal trigger. Euro adoption by 2030 is government policy. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans, legacy bank taxes, and Swiss franc-denominated household debt. Eastern Europe's fiscal pressures — war, energy costs, political transition — are being framed as a regional cluster problem (Reuters, May 7). A severe drought threatens agricultural output, reported in isolation from fiscal transition pressures.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — remains the defining rule-of-law story (Reuters, Guardian, AP, WaPo, Euronews, Notes from Poland). Poland is now seeking US clarification on Ziobro's entry. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier assets. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. The justice minister nominee's LGBTQ commitment is the first concrete government-agenda signal on minority rights. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**Reuters policy agenda synthesis** (May 13): First consolidated international overview of the Magyar government's full policy programme — EU funds, euro adoption, judicial reform, LGBTQ revision, Ukraine normalisation, institutional restructuring — marking the transition from inauguration coverage to governance accountability coverage.

**Supermajority as governance design risk**: Visegrad Insight and The Times frame the two-thirds majority not as democratic strength but as a structural threat to checks and balances — the central tension now replacing "will Magyar win?" with "can Magyar govern without replicating Orbán's power concentration?"

**Fidesz regional collapse** (Balkan Insight, May 8): Orbán's defeat is forcing Hungarian minority parties in Serbia and the Balkans to restructure — the first substantive reporting on Fidesz's regional network unravelling beyond Hungary's borders.

**Banking as authoritarian infrastructure** (Euractiv, May 8): Orbán's use of banking regulation as a political tool is being analysed as structural legacy rather than incidental policy — framing financial sector reform as a rule-of-law issue, not merely an economic one.
