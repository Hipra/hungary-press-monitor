## Political situation
Magyar's Tisza Party government (two-thirds majority, formed April 20) is 16 days old. Dominant tension: democratic restoration vs. early consolidation. Magyar is defending his brother-in-law's appointment as justice minister and a close adviser in an "enforcer role" — the first sustained rule-of-law allegations directed at Magyar personally (Euronews, Politico, May 1). Politico (May 4) warns Western enthusiasm for Magyar risks reproducing the uncritical observation that failed to catch Orbán's early consolidation in 2010. Anti-corruption enforcement is accelerating: police froze assets and accounts of an Orbán-era media mogul (Reuters, May 6). Hungarian voters identified corruption as the primary cause of Orbán's fall (EUobserver, Apr 30). Euractiv (May 6) elevated Budapest as "living proof" that European cities remain the heartland of democracy.

## Geopolitics
Hungary lifted its Ukraine loan veto; Magyar proposed a June bilateral with Zelenskyy on minority rights. Zelenskyy is pressing for full EU accession rather than symbolic membership, exposing structural enlargement friction beyond Hungary's removed veto (FT, Euronews, May 1). Magyar's government is committing to euro adoption by 2030, reversing Orbán's decade-long resistance (DW, May 6). Le Monde frames Orbán's defeat as a strategic loss for Russia. Politico (May 3) folds Hungary's realignment into a broader Putin "window of opportunity" narrative. The Orbán–Fico alliance has ended; MEPs warned Fico that Slovakia risks becoming the next EU funds casualty. Fidesz's international influence networks — built over 15 years — are now unraveling as regional partners distance themselves (Balkan Insight, May 6). US-Hungary relations remain entirely absent from coverage.

## Economy
Magyar is targeting a May deal to unlock ~€10bn in frozen EU funds; Brussels negotiations concluded Apr 26–29. BBC and The Times quote Magyar saying funds will arrive "soon," but FT and Euractiv confirm firm conditionality is maintained. The EU is taking Hungary to court over an Orbán-era retail tax, establishing state continuity liability regardless of political transition (FT, Apr 29). Hungary and Slovakia were separately warned over discriminatory fuel pricing. Hungary's business elite is pivoting toward EU reintegration; banks are publicly calling for a level regulatory playing field from Magyar's government (Reuters, May 5). Magyar's euro-by-2030 commitment signals the deepest monetary integration pivot yet (DW, May 6). V4 economies showing resilience amid Hormuz disruptions.

## Rule of law & human rights
The brother-in-law justice minister appointment has crystallized a nepotism counter-narrative against Magyar. Police asset freezes against an Orbán-era media mogul mark the first concrete criminal enforcement action beyond regulatory pressure (Reuters, May 6). At least one prominent oligarch has capitulated under regulatory and legal scrutiny (Euractiv, May 6). A media mogul who designed Orbán's campaigns offered his firms to the state (Reuters, May 5) — first concrete ownership shift, editorial independence mechanics unresolved. Hungary's competition watchdog launched a probe into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets (Reuters, May 5). Hungary remains among the EU's worst press freedom offenders alongside Greece and Cyprus (EUobserver, Apr 30). Orbán is reportedly seeking an EU Parliament seat for legal immunity (EUobserver, Apr 28). DW (May 1) raised Orbán accountability — prosecution pathways remain unclear.

## Emerging narratives
**Criminal enforcement crosses into asset seizure** (Reuters, May 6): Police freezing assets and bank accounts of an Orbán-era media mogul escalates enforcement from regulatory pressure to criminal-grade action — a qualitative shift in the anti-corruption campaign's legal intensity and reach.

**Euro-by-2030 commitment** (DW, May 6): Magyar's government has announced a concrete eurozone accession target, the most structurally significant monetary policy reversal since Orbán took power. This transforms Hungary from EU outlier to integration candidate on a defined timeline.

**Fidesz international network unraveling** (Balkan Insight, May 6): With state resources gone and regional partners distancing, Fidesz's carefully constructed 15-year influence architecture across the Visegrád Group and beyond faces systematic collapse — a distinct storyline from Orbán's personal fate.

**Orbán consolidation harder to reverse than communism** (Euractiv, May 5): A 1989 democratic transition veteran's framing — that ousting Orbán proved harder than defeating Soviet communism — is entering the interpretive canon, signaling a historiographical shift in how the transition is being narrated internationally.
