## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as PM on May 10–11, 2026, ending Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a Roma children campaign promise on inauguration day and appointed Hungary's first blind minister. The justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. The first cabinet crisis emerged when Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid following nepotism criticism — now a durable counter-narrative in Western outlets. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation. Euractiv (May 13) frames the new cabinet as signaling a sharp institutional break with Orbán-era governance. The supermajority is increasingly framed as structural risk, not democratic strength. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat and offered to resign as party chief.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–12, covered across WaPo, BBC, Guardian, DW, Le Monde, Euronews. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Hungary confirmed a Paks nuclear plant expansion review. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. NYT reports Magyar's government is scrutinising Orbán's China ties. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage. Euractiv signals Hungary may raise billions in additional Ukrainian military aid repayments.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved: Reuters (May 11) characterises the goal as "ambitious but possible," while Euractiv (May 7) counters with "this deal is getting worse all the time" — two competing frames, neither resolved. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but flagged euro-adoption steps as a potential reversal trigger. Euro adoption by 2030 is government policy. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans, legacy bank taxes, and Swiss franc-denominated household debt. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — covered in isolation, not yet linked to fiscal transition pressures.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — remains the defining rule-of-law story (Reuters, Guardian, AP, WaPo, Euronews, Notes from Poland). Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier assets. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. The justice minister nominee's LGBTQ commitment is the first concrete government-agenda signal on minority rights. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**New economic governance model** (Reuters, May 12): Magyar's ministerial veto structure is being framed not just as a cabinet management tool but as a deliberate institutional design choice — distributing power horizontally rather than concentrating it, explicitly contrasting with Orbán's governing style.

**Voter mandate specifics** (Guardian, May 7): Poll data shows Magyar's voters prioritise climate action and LGBTQ+ rights — the first concrete data on reform expectations from the electorate, not just elite commentary.

**China exposure scrutiny** (NYT, May 8): Magyar's government actively reviewing Orbán's China ties — first substantive reporting on Beijing's diminished leverage post-transition, distinct from the Russia realignment story.

**Várhelyi cleared** (Euronews/Euractiv, May 8): European Commission cleared Hungary's former EU Commissioner of spying allegations — a procedural story signalling EU institutional independence functioning during transition.
