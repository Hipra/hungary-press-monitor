## Political situation
Magyar's Tisza Party government (two-thirds majority, formed April 20) is now 15 days old. The bifurcated narrative sharpens: democratic restoration vs. power consolidation. Magyar is defending his brother-in-law's appointment as justice minister and a close adviser in an "enforcer role" (Euronews, Politico, May 1; Euractiv, Apr 28) — the first named rule-of-law allegations directed at Magyar personally. Anti-corruption enforcement is active: asset transfers by Orbán-linked individuals were blocked (Reuters, Apr 28). A Politico (May 4) analysis warns European observers are missing blind spots in their assessment of Magyar's consolidation patterns. Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat (Apr 25) and offered to resign as Fidesz chief. A veteran of 1989 tells Euractiv that ousting Orbán was harder than defeating communism (May 5).

## Geopolitics
Hungary lifted its Ukraine loan veto; Magyar proposed a June bilateral with Zelenskyy on minority rights (Reuters, Le Monde, Apr 28). Zelenskyy is pushing for full EU accession rather than symbolic membership, exposing structural enlargement friction beyond Hungary's removed veto (FT, Euronews, May 1). Le Monde frames Orbán's defeat as a strategic loss for Russia (Apr 29). Politico (May 3) folds Hungary's realignment into a broader Putin "window of opportunity" narrative. The Orbán–Fico alliance has ended; MEPs warned Fico that Slovakia risks becoming the next EU funds casualty (EUobserver, Apr 30). Hungary is expanding solar capacity as a strategic break from Russian energy dependence (DW, Apr 28). US-Hungary relations remain entirely absent from coverage.

## Economy
Magyar is targeting a May deal to unlock ~€10bn in frozen EU funds; Brussels negotiations concluded Apr 26–29. BBC and The Times quote Magyar saying funds will arrive "soon," but FT and Euractiv confirm firm conditionality is maintained. The EU is taking Hungary to court over an Orbán-era retail tax — establishing state continuity liability regardless of transition (FT, Apr 29). Hungary and Slovakia were separately warned over discriminatory fuel pricing (FT, Apr 29). Hungary's business elite is pivoting toward EU reintegration (FT, Apr 28); Moody's assessed the transition as credit positive. Banks are publicly calling for a level regulatory playing field from Magyar's government (Reuters, May 5). V4 economies showing resilience amid Hormuz disruptions (Visegrad Insight, Apr 29).

## Rule of law & human rights
The brother-in-law justice minister appointment has crystallized a nepotism counter-narrative against Magyar. Balkan Insight (May 4) identifies media freedom reform as the most urgent structural priority. A media mogul who designed Orbán's campaigns offered his firms to the state (Reuters, May 5) — the first concrete ownership shift, but editorial independence mechanics remain unresolved. Hungary remains among the EU's worst press freedom offenders (EUobserver, Apr 30). DW (May 1) examines accountability for Orbán's system — prosecution pathways remain unclear. Orbán is reportedly seeking an EU Parliament seat for legal immunity (EUobserver, Apr 28). Far-right think-tank financing networks persist beyond the transition (EUobserver, Apr 28). DW documents cautious Romani optimism (Apr 27–28).

## Emerging narratives
**Magyar's blind spots** (Politico, May 4): First substantive international analysis questioning whether European observers are too optimistic about Magyar — frames early consolidation patterns as underreported. **Hungary as democratic export model** (DW, Apr 29): Hungary's transition is actively inspiring Israel's opposition against Netanyahu — first documented instance of the Hungarian model applied as foreign political playbook. **1989 comparison** (Euractiv, May 5): A 1989 transition veteran argues Orbán's removal was harder than defeating communism, deepening the historical framing of the transition's significance. **Central Europe as political laboratory** (The Guardian, May 4): Hungary folded into a broader CEE democratic-contestation frame alongside Poland and Slovakia.
