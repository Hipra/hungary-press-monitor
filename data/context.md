## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as Prime Minister on May 10–11, 2026, formally ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a campaign promise benefiting Roma children on inauguration day. Hungary's first blind minister was appointed. The justice minister nominee (May 12) committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. A first cabinet crisis emerged when Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid following nepotism criticism — the same nepotism narrative (Euractiv, May 12) is now a standalone governance-risk story. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán's resignation offer as party chief is unresolved. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more" — the sharpest geopolitical self-characterisation from the new government to date.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–12 (WaPo, BBC, Guardian, DW, Le Monde, Euronews), cementing reintegration as concrete and vote-trackable. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Hungary confirmed a review of the Paks nuclear plant expansion — the clearest energy-sector realignment signal. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved: Reuters (May 11) reports officials now describe accessing the funds as "ambitious but possible" — a tonal shift from earlier characterisations of a deteriorating standoff. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but signalled euro-adoption steps could improve the rating. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and a legacy bank tax. Swiss franc-denominated household debt remains a growth drag. War and energy costs compound Eastern Europe's deficit management challenges. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — an emerging secondary fiscal pressure not yet linked in coverage to the broader transition.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar declared Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — is the defining rule-of-law story of the week (Reuters, Guardian, Euractiv, AP, WaPo, Euronews); Poland is demanding answers. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; at least one oligarch capitulated under scrutiny; a campaign-designer media mogul offered his firms to the state. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. A competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. The justice minister nominee's public commitment to LGBTQ legislative revision is the first concrete government-agenda signal. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**"Moscow's Trojan horse no more"** (The Times, May 12): A Hungarian minister's explicit self-declaration marks the sharpest verbal break from Orbán-era Russia alignment yet — moving geopolitical repositioning from action (Paks review, parade absence) to official rhetoric.

**Nepotism as standalone crisis** (Euractiv, Politico, May 8–12): The brother-in-law justice minister withdrawal and subsequent nepotism-clouds-transition framing have consolidated into a durable counter-narrative that rivals the democratic-restoration story in Western outlets.

**Supermajority as democratic risk** (Visegrad Insight, The Times, May 9–12): The Tisza Party's two-thirds majority is increasingly framed not as democratic strength but as structural replication of Orbán-era executive concentration.

**Fidesz regional network unraveling** (Balkan Insight, May 6–8): Orbán's defeat is forcing Hungarian-aligned parties in Serbia and broader CEE to recalibrate; Fidesz's international influence architecture is in active dissolution.

**China scrutiny as standalone story** (NYT, May 7–8): The new government's active examination of Orbán's China relationships has emerged as a distinct narrative, separate from Russia coverage, signalling a broader geopolitical audit of inherited partnerships.
