## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as PM on May 10–11, 2026, ending Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; the government delivered a Roma children campaign promise on inauguration day and appointed Hungary's first blind minister. The justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. The first cabinet crisis — Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid amid nepotism criticism — remains a durable counter-narrative in Western outlets. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation, framed as deliberate horizontal power distribution. The supermajority is increasingly framed as structural governance risk. Coverage is transitioning from inauguration celebration to accountability scrutiny. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat and offered to resign as party chief.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–13. Hungary summoned the Russian ambassador to protest drone strikes on western Ukraine, the sharpest direct anti-Moscow signal yet from the Magyar government. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Euractiv signals Hungary may raise billions in additional Ukrainian military aid repayments. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. NYT reports scrutiny of Orbán's China ties. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved: Reuters (May 11) characterises the goal as "ambitious but possible," while Euractiv (May 7) counters with "this deal is getting worse all the time." An Estonian minister reaffirmed non-negotiable EU conditionality on fund access (Euronews, May 13). S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but flagged euro-adoption steps as a potential reversal trigger. Euro adoption by 2030 is government policy. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans, legacy bank taxes, and Swiss franc-denominated household debt. A severe drought threatens agricultural output, reported in isolation from fiscal pressures.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — remains the defining rule-of-law story (Reuters, Guardian, AP, WaPo, Euronews, Notes from Poland). Poland is now seeking US clarification on Ziobro's entry. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier assets. The FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. The Guardian critically questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**Russian ambassador summoned** (Reuters, May 13): Hungary's direct diplomatic protest over drone strikes on western Ukraine is the first active anti-Moscow démarche by the Magyar government — escalating beyond symbolic non-attendance at the Victory Day parade into formal diplomatic confrontation.

**Governance accountability phase begins** (Reuters/Guardian, May 13): International coverage is explicitly transitioning from inauguration euphoria to scrutiny of whether Magyar can deliver — The Guardian's "Can Magyar deliver?" and Reuters' policy synthesis mark the shift from transition reporting to governance accountability journalism.

**Visegrad Insight as strategic template** (May 13): Hungary's transition is being reframed not just as a national story but as a replicable model for European democratic security — positioning Magyar's government as a proof-of-concept with lessons for the continent.
