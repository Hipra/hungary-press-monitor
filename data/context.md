## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as Prime Minister on May 10, 2026, formally ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's first cabinet crisis — brother-in-law nominated then withdrawn as justice minister — was resolved before inauguration. Magyar appointed Hungary's first blind minister, signaling merit-based governance intent. DW confirms the new government is moving swiftly on systemic institutional reform. Western scrutiny is hardening: Politico, EUobserver, and ECFR warn against uncritical support, citing nepotism signals and populist tendencies. A 1989 transition veteran told Euractiv that ousting Orbán was harder than defeating communism. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán's resignation offer as party chief is unresolved. May 10 inauguration generated the most coordinated single-day international coverage of the transition — BBC, WaPo, AP, Reuters, NYT, Guardian, Le Monde, Euronews, Euractiv, DW, and Politico converging on "regime change" framing.

## Geopolitics
Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Kyiv hailed it as qualitative normalisation. Magyar has proposed a June bilateral with Zelenskyy on minority rights. Hungary's attendance at Putin's Victory Day parade remains tracked as a concrete Russia-policy signal. Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome, establishing Italy as Hungary's primary EU peer partner. Euro adoption by 2030 committed. NYT and Magyar's government are now actively scrutinising Orbán's China relationships — a concrete policy signal of Beijing realignment. Fidesz's V4 and Balkan influence networks are actively unraveling; Hungarian-aligned parties in Serbia face strategic recalibration. Le Monde frames European strategic autonomisation driven by Ukraine war pressure as reshaping Hungary's reintegration context. Euractiv flags a potential early clash between Magyar and Brussels over migration pact compliance. US-Hungary relations remain entirely absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package have deteriorated; Euractiv characterises the standoff as worsening — post-election optimism has given way to technical gridlock over conditionality benchmarks. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks, but signalled euro-adoption steps could improve the rating. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. Banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and a legacy bank tax, with banks publicly calling for a level playing field. Swiss franc-denominated household debt identified as a structural growth drag. War and energy costs compound Eastern Europe's deficit management challenge. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — an emerging secondary pressure on the new government's economic agenda not yet connected in reporting to the fiscal transition.

## Rule of law & human rights
Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree — a concrete judicial independence signal. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; at least one oligarch capitulated under scrutiny; a campaign-designer media mogul offered his firms to the state. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. Competition watchdog launched a probe into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine, characterising state broadcasting as a "factory of lies." Balkan Insight confirmed Tisza's victory creates a historic media freedom reform opportunity. Public demand for Orbán's personal criminal prosecution is intensifying (EUobserver); legal basis and timeline remain unreported. Guardian poll confirms voter demand for LGBTQ+ and climate reform; legislative pathway absent. EU cleared Várhelyi of Brussels spying probe.
