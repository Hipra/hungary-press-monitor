## Political situation
Péter Magyar's Tisza Party has secured a confirmed two-thirds parliamentary majority following the final vote count, formally ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. Magyar is internationally characterized as "a man in a hurry," advancing his reform agenda at pace. The Guardian identifies his decisive strategic innovation: converting loyal rural Fidesz voters rather than mobilizing traditional opposition constituencies, abandoning left-wing messaging to position himself as the patriotic alternative. No specific cabinet appointments, legislative calendar, or early governing decisions have yet been reported in international press. The Economist raises the novel question of whether Orbán can stage a comeback as an opposition figure.

## Geopolitics
Hungary's geopolitical alignment has shifted fundamentally. EU officials are already in Budapest conducting high-stakes talks on democratic reforms and reintegration terms. Magyar has engaged Poland as a primary partner for rebuilding EU relations. Orbán's defeat is widely framed as a strategic loss for Moscow, ending Hungary's role as Russia's primary NATO ally in Central Europe. Ukrainian observers frame the result as "a defeat for Putin." Washington Post identifies Bulgaria as Russia's emerging fallback Central European partner. US-Hungary relations under the Trump administration remain entirely unreported. V4 dynamics following Hungary's EU pivot have not been addressed.

## Economy
International investors are significantly increasing purchases of Hungarian assets, directly pricing in closer EU integration and improved economic governance under Magyar (Financial Times). Capital inflows are driven by expectations of restored EU fund access and rule-of-law compliance. ## Rule of law & human rights
The Financial Times identifies judicial reform as one of Magyar's central governing challenges, alongside EU reintegration and geopolitical realignment. EU reintegration talks are expected to include rule-of-law compliance as a precondition. The Times investigation positions Orbán's personal enrichment — specifically a mansion in his home village — as a direct electoral cause of his downfall rather than mere background corruption.
