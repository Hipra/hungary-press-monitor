## Political situation

Péter Magyar was sworn in May 10–11, 2026, ending Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority; the full cabinet took office May 12. Magyar symbolically removed fences around Orbán's former offices and opened his palaces to guided public tours. The wartime state of emergency was formally ended. A nepotism cabinet crisis — Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid — remains an active counter-narrative. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation. Coverage has shifted from inauguration celebration to delivery scrutiny; supermajority concentration risks draw sustained critical attention. Politico (May 18) now frames Hungary as a potential EU peacemaker. Ukraine-Hungary consultations on Hungarian minorities began May 18, advancing Magyar's bilateral reset with Kyiv.

## Geopolitics

Hungary summoned the Russian ambassador to protest drone strikes near its border; a minister declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers, ending Orbán's habitual veto. Hungary returned $82 million to Ukrainian citizens; bilateral consultations on Hungarian minorities in Ukraine launched May 18. Hungary joined 36 countries approving a special tribunal for Russian war crimes. However, Euractiv (May 17) reports EU member states are actively frustrated with Budapest over Ukraine aid blockages — the key counter-narrative to EU reintegration. The Times (May 17) flags Hungarian minorities in Ukraine facing new security risks under Magyar's pro-EU pivot. A leaked call confirmed Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs evade sanctions. US-Hungary relations remain absent from international coverage.

## Economy

Magyar is preparing a letter to von der Leyen outlining Hungary's red lines in EU funding talks; an EU negotiating team has arrived in Budapest. Reuters characterises the €10.4bn access goal as "ambitious but possible"; an Estonian minister reaffirmed non-negotiable conditionality. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks. Euro adoption by 2030 remains government policy. The non-EU worker visa halt (Reuters, May 15) is generating business-sector alarm over labor supply. A severe drought constrains bioethanol expansion due to maize shortages. Hundreds of Hungarian roads may be contaminated with asbestos, raising unquantified infrastructure liability. Serbia submitted a final proposal on NIS assets to Hungary's MOL, signaling regional energy realignment.

## Rule of law & human rights

The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister who fled Hungary to the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — remains the defining rule-of-law story. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier assets. Reuters frames asset freezes as active, accelerating institutional dismantling of Orbán's media empire. The Guardian critically questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Hungary's justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation; the 2026 Rainbow Map reflects Hungary as improving-but-unresolved. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying. Judicial appointment reform and prosecution timelines remain unreported internationally.
