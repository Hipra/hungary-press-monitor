## Political situation

Péter Magyar was sworn in May 10–11, 2026, ending Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority; the full cabinet took office May 12. Magyar opened Orbán's former palaces to guided public tours and ended the wartime state of emergency. A nepotism cabinet crisis — Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid — remains an active counter-narrative. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation. Coverage has shifted from inauguration to delivery scrutiny; supermajority concentration risks draw sustained critical attention. A new institutional tension emerged May 18: Hungary's president is resisting Magyar's calls to resign, signaling conflict over presidential powers within the post-transition government. Roma representation and minority inclusion are receiving unusually prominent coverage as symbolic markers of democratic restoration.

## Geopolitics

Hungary-Ukraine bilateral consultations on Hungarian minority rights launched May 18, the first concrete diplomatic event under Magyar's government. Magyar signalled openness to supporting Ukraine's EU accession — a complete reversal of Orbán-era obstruction. Hungary summoned the Russian ambassador over drone strikes near its border; a minister declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers, joined 36 countries approving a special tribunal for Russian war crimes, and returned $82 million to Ukrainian citizens. However, Euractiv (May 17) reports EU member states remain actively frustrated with Budapest over Ukraine aid blockages — the key counter-narrative to EU reintegration. The Times (May 17) flags Hungarian minorities in Ukraine facing new security risks under Magyar's pro-EU pivot. Slovakia under Fico is now framed as Hungary's successor as EU's principal disruptor. US-Hungary relations remain absent from international coverage.

## Economy

Magyar is preparing a letter to von der Leyen outlining Hungary's red lines in EU funding talks; an EU negotiating team has arrived in Budapest. Reuters characterises the €10.4bn access goal as "ambitious but possible"; an Estonian minister reaffirmed non-negotiable conditionality. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks. Euro adoption by 2030 remains government policy. The non-EU worker visa halt (Reuters, May 15) is generating business-sector alarm over labor supply. A severe drought constrains bioethanol expansion due to maize shortages. Hundreds of Hungarian roads may be contaminated with asbestos, raising unquantified infrastructure liability. Serbia submitted a final proposal on NIS assets to Hungary's MOL, signaling regional energy realignment.

## Rule of law & human rights

The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister who fled Hungary to the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — remains the defining rule-of-law story. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier assets. Reuters frames asset freezes as active, accelerating institutional dismantling of Orbán's media empire. The Guardian critically questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Hungary's justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation; the 2026 Rainbow Map reflects Hungary as improving-but-unresolved. Roma children performed the Roma hymn at parliament — a symbolic minority rights milestone. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying. Judicial appointment reform and prosecution timelines remain unreported internationally.
