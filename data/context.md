## Political situation
Magyar's Tisza Party government (two-thirds majority, formed April 20) is 18 days old and entering its formal inauguration phase — Washington Post, AP, and Le Monde (May 8) report Magyar is planning a "regime-change celebration" marking the end of Orbán's 15-year rule, framed by Le Monde as a "democratic spring." The brother-in-law justice minister appointment remains the primary nepotism critique directed at Magyar personally. Politico's sustained critical watchdog posture ("No blank check," "What Europe is missing about Magyar") warns against repeating blind-spot failures that missed Orbán's 2010 consolidation. ECFR published six strategic insights framing democratic consolidation and EU reintegration as defining axes. Magyar's appointment of Hungary's first blind minister signals merit-based governance. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán's resignation offer as party chief is unresolved with zero reported internal succession candidates.

## Geopolitics
Hungary returned $82 million in cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens — Kyiv described it as qualitative normalisation beyond veto-lifting (Reuters, AP, WaPo, Politico, Euronews, May 6). Magyar proposed a June bilateral with Zelenskyy on minority rights. Zelenskyy continues pressing for full EU accession, exposing structural enlargement friction (FT, May 1). Magyar committed to euro adoption by 2030 (DW, May 6) and cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome, establishing Italy as Hungary's primary peer partner in the EU reset (Politico, AP, May 7). NYT frames the election as exposing limits of China's political influence in Central Europe. Fidesz's V4 influence network is actively unraveling (Balkan Insight, May 6). Orbán reportedly seeking EU Parliament seat for immunity. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
Hungary and the EU are in a deteriorating dispute over the exact amount of the ~€10bn frozen funding package; Euractiv (May 7) characterises the standoff as worsening — initial post-election optimism is giving way to technical gridlock over conditionality benchmarks. S&P Global assigned a negative credit rating outlook citing fiscal transition risks (Reuters, May 6). Offsetting this, German investor morale improved in a post-election survey (Reuters, May 7). Hungary's banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and a bank tax; banks are calling for a level regulatory playing field (Reuters, May 5). Swiss franc-denominated household debt identified as a structural "slow poison" for growth (Reuters, May 7). Euro-by-2030 commitment signals the deepest monetary integration pivot yet; fiscal convergence mechanics remain unreported.

## Rule of law & human rights
The FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker (May 7) systematically maps Orbán-era crony asset seizures, regulatory probes, and forced divestitures as an ongoing investigative beat. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul (Reuters, FT, May 6); at least one oligarch has capitulated under scrutiny (Euractiv, May 6). A campaign-designer media mogul offered his firms to the state; editorial independence mechanics remain unresolved (Reuters, May 5). Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree — the first concrete judicial independence signal (Euractiv, May 7). The Guardian directly questions whether state media reform will be genuine (May 6). Public demand for Orbán's personal criminal prosecution is intensifying as a distinct story (EUobserver, May 7); prosecution timelines and legal basis remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**Inauguration as symbolic rupture** (WaPo, AP, Le Monde, May 8): Magyar's planned "regime-change celebration" and Le Monde's "democratic spring" framing mark a new phase — the inauguration as historical milestone, not just governmental transition. This celebratory register is new in international coverage.

**Swiss franc debt as hidden growth liability** (Reuters, May 7): First appearance of currency-denominated household debt as a structural economic risk distinct from fiscal or EU-funding stories — a new macro vulnerability thread.

**Central Europe as political laboratory** (The Guardian letters, May 4): Hungary's transition being explicitly positioned alongside Poland and Slovakia as a regional case study in democratic contestation, not just a Hungary-specific story.
