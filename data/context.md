## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as Prime Minister on May 10–11, 2026, ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a Roma children campaign promise on inauguration day and appointed Hungary's first blind minister. The justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. The first cabinet crisis emerged when Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid following nepotism criticism — now a durable counter-narrative in Western outlets. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation, signaling a new governance model. The supermajority is increasingly framed as structural risk, not democratic strength. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat and offered to resign as party chief.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–12, covered across WaPo, BBC, Guardian, DW, Le Monde, Euronews — cementing EU reintegration as vote-trackable. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Hungary confirmed a review of the Paks nuclear plant expansion. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. NYT reports Magyar's government is scrutinising Orbán's China ties. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved; Reuters (May 11) characterises the goal as "ambitious but possible" — a tonal shift, though conditionality disputes persist. Euractiv (May 7) separately signals deterioration in talks. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but flagged euro-adoption steps as a potential reversal trigger. Euro adoption by 2030 is government policy. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and legacy bank taxes. Swiss franc-denominated household debt remains a growth drag. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — an emerging secondary fiscal pressure not yet linked in coverage to the broader economic transition.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — is the defining rule-of-law story of the week (Reuters, Guardian, AP, WaPo, Euronews, Notes from Poland). Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. The justice minister nominee's LGBTQ commitment is the first concrete government-agenda signal. A Guardian poll shows Magyar voters prioritise climate action and LGBTQ+ rights. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**Voter mandate for progressive reform** (Guardian, May 7): First polling data showing Magyar's voters specifically prioritise climate action and LGBTQ+ rights — moves the conversation from elite institutional reform to bottom-up democratic demand, a new frame absent from earlier coverage.

**Euractiv conditionality deterioration** (May 7): A second, contradictory EU funds signal — "this deal is getting worse all the time" — sits in tension with Reuters' "ambitious but possible" (May 11). Two competing frames on the same negotiation now active simultaneously; neither has resolved.

**Várhelyi exoneration** (Euronews/Euractiv, May 8): European Commission clears Hungary's former EU Commissioner Olivér Várhelyi of spying allegations — a quiet institutional signal that EU oversight is functioning independently of the political transition, potentially relevant to conditionality credibility.

**European far-right resilience framing** (Guardian/Parliament Magazine, May 6–7): Multiple outlets explicitly argue Orbán's fall does not signal far-right decline in Europe — a counter-narrative to the "democratic spring" frame, gaining traction as a structural analytical lens.
