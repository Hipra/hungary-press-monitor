## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as Prime Minister on May 10–11, 2026, formally ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a campaign promise benefiting Roma children on inauguration day. Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — a concrete foreign policy break from Orbán-era vetoes. The brother-in-law justice minister crisis was resolved before the ceremony. Hungary's first blind minister was appointed. Western scrutiny is hardening: Politico, EUobserver, and ECFR warn against uncritical support, citing nepotism signals and populist tendencies. The Times questions whether the supermajority concentrates dangerous executive power. The EU flag was restored to parliament. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán's resignation offer as party chief remains unresolved. DW frames the central question as whether Magyar can dismantle Orbán's institutional system, not merely replace its personnel.

## Geopolitics
Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation and Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome, establishing Italy as Hungary's primary EU peer partner. Euro adoption by 2030 committed. Magyar's government announced a review of the Paks nuclear plant expansion — the clearest energy-sector signal of geopolitical realignment to date — with a ministerial nominee formally confirming the review on May 11. Magyar's government is scrutinising Orbán's China relationships. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals how Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. Fidesz's V4 and Balkan influence networks are actively unraveling. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package show mixed signals: Euractiv characterises the standoff as deteriorating, but Reuters (May 11) reports officials now describe accessing the funds as "ambitious but possible" — a notable tonal shift introducing cautious optimism. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks, but signalled euro-adoption steps could improve the rating. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and a legacy bank tax; banks are calling for a level playing field under the new government. Swiss franc-denominated household debt remains a structural growth drag. War and energy costs compound Eastern Europe's deficit management challenges. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — an emerging secondary fiscal pressure not yet connected in reporting to the broader transition.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — a former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar declared Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — consolidated into the week's defining rule-of-law story, covered across Reuters, Guardian, Euractiv, AP, WaPo, and Euronews. Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; at least one oligarch capitulated under scrutiny; a campaign-designer media mogul offered his firms to the state. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. A competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. Public demand for Orbán's personal criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported. EU Commissioner Várhelyi was cleared of wrongdoing in a Brussels spying probe.

## Emerging narratives
**EU foreign policy alignment as proof-of-concept** (Euronews, May 11): Hungary's backing of EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — previously blocked under Orbán — is emerging as a concrete, vote-trackable measure of EU reintegration beyond symbolism. First appearance in coverage.

**Viral inauguration imagery as political signal** (WaPo, Guardian, AP, May 10–11): Magyar dancing with MPs and the health minister dancing outside parliament received prominent placement in US and UK outlets, framing the cultural reset as globally legible. The "joyful Budapest" register (ECFR) is now a standalone editorial theme.

**Guardian fascism question** (Guardian, May 11): A Guardian podcast explicitly asks whether Hungary's transition signals a return of fascism — the first time this framing appears, contrasting sharply with the dominant democratic-restoration narrative and potentially opening a new critical register.

**Paks nuclear review as geopolitical rupture** (Reuters, May 11): First concrete energy-sector signal of Russia-China disentanglement — a ministerial nominee confirmed Hungary will review the Orbán-era Paks expansion deal, giving the abstract China-Russia scrutiny a specific high-stakes infrastructure target.
