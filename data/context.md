## Political situation
Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority; cabinet announced April 20. Magyar is pressing Hungary's president and top judges to resign as part of judicial renewal. Agriculture ministry appointment of a corporate executive contradicts small-farm campaign promises — Magyar's first credibility gap (Euractiv, Apr 21). State media broadcast suspension announced. Misinformation against Magyar persists despite electoral victory (Euronews, Apr 23). Magyar revealed his strategy for avoiding Orbán-era kompromat — framing political survival as deliberate counter-intelligence discipline (Euractiv, Apr 24). Foreign Policy (Apr 24) published dedicated analysis of institutional and strategic pathways for rebuilding Hungarian democracy. DW (Apr 24) identifies three structural hurdles: ECJ LGBTQ ruling requiring legislation, entrenched Orbán-era institutions, and EU fund conditionality. EU capitals have shifted posture from celebration to managed patience.

## Emerging narratives
**"Post-euphoria" structural realism**: Politico (Apr 24) argues EU capitals can no longer use Hungary as a scapegoat for deeper systemic problems — shifting register from celebrating Orbán's defeat to confronting harder institutional and competitiveness challenges. **Orbán exile speculation**: Balkan Insight (Apr 24) reports Orbán considering US exile — first concrete reporting on his personal post-defeat trajectory, elevating accountability and legal exposure questions. **Magyar's kompromat strategy**: Euractiv (Apr 24) introduces new frame — Magyar's political survival as deliberate counter-intelligence discipline, not just electoral momentum. **Left's structural decline in CEE**: Le Monde (Apr 24) surfaces a paradox: Orbán fell, but the left is receding across Central Europe — suggesting Hungary's transition reflects liberal rather than left-wing realignment. **Far-right fragmentation opportunity**: The Parliament Magazine (Apr 24) argues European liberal forces should strategically exploit internal far-right discord, framing Hungary's transition as a systemic opportunity. **"Undoing Orbán without becoming Orbán"**: EUobserver (Apr 21) names the institutional paradox — overzealous reform risks replicating the concentrated power it seeks to dismantle.
