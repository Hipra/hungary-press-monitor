## Political situation
Hungary has completed a historic democratic transition following the April 2026 elections. Péter Magyar's Tisza Party secured a two-thirds parliamentary majority, ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. Magyar has assumed the premiership and is moving rapidly on his reform agenda. The final vote count confirms the majority is robust. Orbán's defeat is attributed partly to Magyar's success in converting loyal rural Fidesz voters rather than mobilizing traditional opposition constituencies. The Economist notes questions remain about Orbán's capacity for a future political comeback as an opposition figure.

## Geopolitics
Hungary's geopolitical alignment has shifted dramatically. Magyar's government is actively seeking EU reintegration, with EU officials already conducting high-stakes talks in Budapest. Magyar has reached out to Poland as a partner in rebuilding EU relations. Orbán's defeat is widely framed as a strategic setback for Moscow, ending Hungary's role as Russia's key NATO ally in Central Europe. Ukrainian observers celebrate the result as "a defeat for Putin." Russia is now assessed as pivoting toward Bulgaria to maintain Central European influence.

## Economy
International investors are piling into Hungarian assets, betting on closer EU integration and improved economic governance under Magyar. The Financial Times reports significant capital inflows following the electoral result. The improved investment climate is directly linked to expectations of restored EU fund access and rule-of-law compliance. No specific fiscal policy announcements or economic crises are yet reported, reflecting the early transition stage.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Financial Times identifies judicial reform as one of Magyar's central battles ahead. EU talks are expected to address democratic reforms and rule-of-law compliance as preconditions for full reintegration. Corruption is prominently in focus: The Times investigates Orbán's personal enrichment, specifically a mansion in his home village, framing it as emblematic of the systemic corruption that contributed to his downfall. No specific media freedom or civil society developments are yet reported post-transition.
