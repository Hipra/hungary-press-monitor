## Political situation
Péter Magyar's Tisza Party has formally consolidated a confirmed two-thirds parliamentary majority following the final vote count, ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. Magyar is internationally characterized as "a man in a hurry," advancing his reform agenda rapidly. The Guardian identifies his decisive strategic innovation: converting loyal rural Fidesz voters rather than mobilizing traditional opposition constituencies, abandoning left-wing messaging to position himself as the patriotic alternative. No specific cabinet appointments or detailed legislative calendar has been reported internationally. The Economist raises the question of whether Orbán can stage a comeback from the opposition bench.

## Geopolitics
EU officials conducted high-stakes Budapest talks on democratic reforms and reintegration; Magyar and the EU agreed to collaborate on releasing frozen EU funds following weekend negotiations. Magyar has engaged Poland as a primary partner for rebuilding EU relations. Orbán's defeat is widely framed as a strategic loss for Moscow; Ukrainian observers call it "a defeat for Putin." Washington Post identifies Bulgaria as Russia's emerging fallback Central European partner. DW frames the result as weakening strongman governance across Eastern Europe broadly. US-Hungary relations under the Trump administration remain entirely unreported. V4 dynamics following Hungary's EU pivot remain unaddressed.

## Economy
Moody's has assessed Magyar's pro-EU Tisza government as credit positive for Hungary, the first formal sovereign credit signal of the transition. International investors are significantly increasing purchases of Hungarian assets, pricing in closer EU integration and improved governance (Financial Times). The EU-Magyar agreement on releasing frozen funds marks the first concrete economic governance milestone. A separate Euractiv report notes richer EU states gaining advantage in the €400bn competitiveness fund distribution — a potential constraint on Hungary's access to EU capital during reintegration. No specific fiscal policy announcements or budget decisions have emerged.

## Rule of law & human rights
Magyar's government announced suspension of state media broadcasts while guaranteeing press freedom — the first concrete institutional media reform reported internationally (Reuters). The Financial Times identifies judicial reform as one of Magyar's central governing challenges alongside EU reintegration. The Times investigation frames Orbán's personal enrichment — a mansion in his home village — as a direct electoral cause of his downfall, keeping corruption in international framing. EU reintegration talks are expected to include rule-of-law compliance as a precondition. No post-transition reporting yet on minority rights, Roma policy, LGBT rights, civil society recovery, or potential legal exposure for Orbán.
