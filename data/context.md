## Political situation
Péter Magyar was sworn in as Prime Minister on May 10–11, 2026, ending Viktor Orbán's 15-year rule. The Tisza Party holds a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Magyar's inaugural speech included a public apology to Orbán-era victims; his government delivered a Roma children campaign promise on inauguration day and appointed Hungary's first blind minister. The justice minister nominee publicly committed to revising anti-LGBTQ legislation. The first cabinet crisis emerged when Magyar's brother-in-law withdrew from the justice minister bid following nepotism criticism — now a durable counter-narrative in Western outlets. Magyar granted key ministers veto power over legislation, signaling a new governance model. Fidesz remains leaderless; Orbán vacated his parliamentary seat and offered to resign as party chief. The supermajority is increasingly framed as structural risk, not democratic strength.

## Geopolitics
Hungary backed EU sanctions on Israeli settlers — the dominant foreign policy story of May 11–12, covered across WaPo, BBC, Guardian, DW, Le Monde, Euronews — cementing EU reintegration as vote-trackable. Hungary returned $82 million in seized cash and gold to Ukrainian citizens; Zelenskyy hailed it as qualitative normalisation; Magyar proposed a June bilateral on minority rights. Hungary confirmed a review of the Paks nuclear plant expansion. Hungary did not attend Putin's Victory Day parade. A Hungarian minister explicitly declared Hungary is "Moscow's Trojan horse no more." Magyar cemented bilateral ties with Meloni in Rome — Italy is Hungary's primary EU peer partner. New York Times reports Magyar's government is scrutinising Orbán's China ties. A leaked phone call (EUobserver) reveals Orbán's Hungary and Fico's Slovakia helped Russian oligarchs. US-Hungary relations remain absent from coverage.

## Economy
EU-Hungary negotiations over the ~€10bn frozen funding package remain unresolved; Reuters (May 11) characterises the goal as "ambitious but possible" — a tonal shift, though conditionality disputes persist. S&P Global assigned a negative credit outlook citing fiscal transition risks but signalled euro-adoption steps could improve the rating. Euro adoption by 2030 is government policy. German investor morale improved in a post-election survey. The banking sector faces structural drag from bad loans and a legacy bank tax. Swiss franc-denominated household debt remains a growth drag. War and energy costs compound Eastern Europe's deficit management challenges. A severe drought threatens agricultural output — an emerging secondary fiscal pressure not yet linked in coverage to the broader economic transition.

## Rule of law & human rights
The Ziobro case — former Polish justice minister granted asylum by Orbán who fled Hungary for the US after Magyar signalled Hungary will not shelter people sought by foreign authorities — is the defining rule-of-law story of the week (Reuters, Guardian, AP, WaPo, Euronews, Notes from Poland). Hungary's Constitutional Court voided key provisions of an Orbán tax decree. Police froze assets of an Orbán-era media mogul; a competition watchdog probe was launched into Indamedia's acquisition of Ringier media assets. FT's "nepo-stocks" tracker maps ongoing crony asset seizures. The Guardian questions whether state media reform will be genuine. The justice minister nominee's LGBTQ commitment is the first concrete government-agenda signal. Public demand for Orbán's criminal prosecution is intensifying; legal basis and timeline remain unreported.

## Emerging narratives
**China scrutiny** (NYT, May 8): Magyar's government is actively reviewing Orbán's China ties — first substantive reporting on Beijing's diminished leverage following the electoral defeat; a new foreign policy dimension beyond the Russia frame.

**Ministerial veto architecture** (Reuters, May 12): Magyar's decision to grant key ministers legislative veto power is a new governance story — the first concrete institutional design signal beyond personnel appointments, raising questions about intra-cabinet power balance.

**"Moscow's Trojan horse no more"** (The Times, May 12): A Hungarian minister's explicit self-declaration moves geopolitical repositioning from action to official rhetoric — the sharpest verbal verbal break from Orbán-era Russia alignment yet.

**Supermajority as democratic risk** (Visegrad Insight, The Times): Routinely framed as structural replication of Orbán-era executive concentration, not democratic strength; Visegrad Insight explicitly argues winning power is easier than limiting it.

**Fidesz regional unravelling** (Balkan Insight): Orbán's fall is forcing Hungarian-aligned parties in Serbia and the Balkans to recalibrate — a regional spillover story absent from mainstream Western outlets.
